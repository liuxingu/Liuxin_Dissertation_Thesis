\section{Overview}
Modern computing is straining under the combined pressures of scale and efficiency, and AI workloads are growing faster than improvements in transistor density and memory bandwidth.
As models get larger and memory sits farther from the processor, transferring data takes longer and consumes more energy.
Traditional copper interconnects stores charge to send a “1” and discharge to send a “0,” so a longer transfer distance require more energy every time they switch. 
They also respond more slowly because the charge has farther to go and more resistance and capacitance to overcome, which increases latency.

Optical interconnects can transfer data by encoding electrical signal onto light with on-chip optical modulator,which can avoid Ohmic loss and enable high bandwidth over long distances.
In the center of these modulation is the nonlinear response originates form the material's own property. 
As the large optical nonlinearities enables control light with another.

Typically, achieving this large modulation typically requires many photons or high field intensities.
Pushing from classical to quantum photonics, where single photons can influence one another’s behavior, requires much stronger interactions.
This is the long term goal for the field of quantum optics to develop solid-state system which is CMOS compatible, uniform, scalable, and strong coupled to nanophotonic modes. 

2D materials (TMDs) are especially attractive due to the following aspects:
These van der Waals semiconductors are indirect bandgap in bulk but transfer to direct bandgap in the monolayer. 
Their exciton binding energies (typically around hunderds of meV
when hBN encapsulated) are orders of magnitude larger than in conventional III–V systems, yielding robust excitonic resonances with large oscillator strengths and strong light–matter coupling at and even room temperature. 
The monolayer’s broken inversion symmetry and strong spin–orbit coupling give valley dependent optical selection rules, enabling helicity selective excitation and potential valley based information encoding in classical and quantum settings.

In addition, their atomic thin nature supports heterogeneous, low temperature BEOL integration with silicon photonics and CMOS electronics. 
This improves precise placement of the active regions at cavity field antinodes, minimizes parasitics to the electrical drivers, and enables compact, energy efficient electro-optic modulators that leverage exciton-mediated changes in absorption and refraction. 
Embedding TMDs in microcavities or photonic crystal resonators can push systems into the strong coupling regime, 
enhancing nonlinear phase shifts per photon and opening a route from high contrast classical modulation to few photon quantum nonlinear optics on a scalable, chip-integrated platform.

This thesis focus on developing systems with large exciton interactions towards giant optical nonlinearity.
Chapter~\ref{chap2} will discuss the background of excitons, exciton-polaritons and the related physics about TMD semiconductors.

Chapter~\ref{chap4} will discuss the approach towards exciton quantum confinement with novel electrostatic superlattice.
Chapter~\ref{chap5} will introduce the developed system with giant optical nonlinearity of Fermi polarons, where the exciton-carrier interaction contributes to this large nonlinear shift.
Chapter~\ref{chap6} will discuss the ongoing project based on Chap.~\ref{chap5} that we leverage cavity engineering for realizing polaron-polariton with enhanced optical nonlinearity.
Chapter~\ref{chap7} summarize the whole thesis and discuss the future directions of the work.
